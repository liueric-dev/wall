# PRODUCT.md — The Wall

> *Working name. May change before launch.*

---

## What It Is

The Wall is a pixel canvas of New York City, where you can doodle on the part of the city around you. Open the app, see the whole city as a living artwork. Tap to draw, and your physical location becomes a small editable area. Your daily pixel budget refills overnight. Older work softens; new work stands out. The city slowly fills with the small art of the people who walk through it.

It is not a social network. It is not a game. It is a creative outlet for bored moments — a place to leave a mark and see what others have left.

## Audience

Anyone who wants to add to the tapestry of NYC.

More specifically: people who reach for their phone in idle moments and want something better than another scroll feed. People who like to draw but don't make time for it. People who feel a quiet attachment to where they live and want a small way to express it.

---

## The Two Core Verbs

Every feature in The Wall must serve one of two actions:

1. **Doodle** — make something, fast and satisfying
2. **Browse** — see what others have made, slow and curious

If a feature doesn't clearly serve one of these, it doesn't belong in the product.

## Design Principles

### 1. Creativity Over Competition
There are no leaderboards, no teams, no winners. Contribution is its own reward. The product celebrates individual expression and collective accumulation, not rivalry.

### 2. Frictionless Drawing
The path from "open the app" to "make a mark" should be measured in seconds, not steps. Drawing should feel like Procreate or Apple Notes — immediate, satisfying, no setup.

### 3. Geographic Identity
You can only draw where you are. The map is not just a backdrop; it is the canvas. Place is the constraint that makes contribution meaningful.

### 4. Anonymous Contribution
Pixels are not attributed to users on the canvas. The wall is a collective artifact, not a portfolio. Identity is lightweight (an anonymous device session) and largely invisible.

### 5. Ambient, Not Addictive
The product is meant to fit into idle moments, not consume them. Bounded engagement — a small ritual, not an infinite scroll — is a feature, not a limitation.

### 6. Constraints Create Meaning
Limited daily budgets, geographic boundaries, and a small color palette are not concessions. They are the design. Every constraint exists because it makes contributions feel more considered.

### 7. The Map Is a Whisper, the Doodles Are the Song
The base map exists for orientation only. It should be visually quiet. Pixel art carries the visual weight of the product.

### 8. Bounded, Not Algorithmic
There is no feed. There is no recommendation engine. There is the city, and there is what people made today. You see what you choose to see by where you wander on the map.

## What The Wall Is NOT

To stay focused, these are explicit non-goals:

- **Not a social network.** No follows, no profiles, no friends list, no DMs.
- **Not a feed.** No algorithmic timeline, no infinite scroll, no notifications pushing you back.
- **Not a competition.** No leaderboards, votes, scores, or rankings.
- **Not a game.** No achievements, levels, missions, or progression systems.
- **Not a marketplace.** No selling user creations, no NFTs, no commission-based artist pages.
- **Not for everyone everywhere.** NYC at launch. Other cities considered only after NYC is thriving.

If a future feature pulls the product toward any of these, it gets rejected.

## Inspirations (and What We Take From Each)

- **r/place** — Collaborative pixel canvases work at scale. Constraints (cooldowns, limited palette, anonymous contribution) drive engagement.
- **Procreate** — Drawing tools should feel invisible. The interaction is the experience.
- **Townscaper** — Generative beauty without competition. People will return to a creative toy with no goals.
- **BeReal** — Time-bound rituals create belonging without addiction.
- **Pokémon Go** — Augmenting real geography with a digital layer creates a richer relationship with place.
- **Foursquare (early days)** — Hyperlocal check-ins prove people enjoy declaring presence.
- **Wordle** — A daily ritual with bounded engagement can be deeply sticky without being predatory.

We borrow mechanics from these, but the product is none of them.

## Open Questions

These are intentionally undecided. They will be resolved through real usage data, not premature commitment.

1. **Persistence model.** Does the wall reset periodically, fade slowly forever, or some hybrid? *To be removed from this document once decided.*
2. **Monetization.** Likely posters/prints of the wall at meaningful moments, post-MVP. Specifics TBD.
3. **Special modes.** Weekly variations like Doodle Day, Mirror Week, Etch Week. Considered for v2+, not MVP.
4. **Cross-city expansion.** Possible after NYC succeeds. Each city would feel like its own product.
5. **Content moderation infrastructure.** Pre-launch decision about how reports, AI flagging, and human escalation work. Not optional, but specifics still being designed.

## What "Done" Looks Like

The Wall is working when:

- A meaningful number of New Yorkers use it weekly (target: 5,000 weekly active in year 1)
- The map shows visible activity in most populated neighborhoods
- People talk about it as a thing they do, not an app they use
- Users return because they want to, not because of notifications
- The aesthetic of the wall feels distinctly itself — recognizable in screenshots
- Vandalism and abuse are managed without dominating the experience

These are the markers of a healthy product. Specific metrics are tools, not goals.
